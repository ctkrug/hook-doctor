How to write a good opening line

A good opening line does one job: it makes the next sentence feel necessary. The first lines people still quote decades later tend to share three concrete habits, and you can check your own sentence against all three.

Start in the middle of something. "Call me Ishmael" and "124 was spiteful" drop you into a voice or a situation already underway. Openings that warm up on weather or backstory ("It was a bright cold day") have to work harder to earn the second line.

Carry the sentence on specific nouns. "The sky above the port was the color of television, tuned to a dead channel" gives you something to see. Abstract nouns like situation, feeling, or reality ask the reader to picture nothing.

Vary your rhythm. Read the first few sentences aloud. If they all run the same length, the paragraph reads flat even when every word is right. A short line after a long one lands.

Questions writers ask

How do I write a good opening line?
Open mid-action or mid-voice, lean on concrete sensory nouns instead of abstractions, and vary your sentence lengths across the first few lines. Paste a draft above to see where yours stands on each.
Is this a first line generator?
No. Hook Doctor does not write the line for you. It scores the line you already wrote and shows the closest match from its corpus of famous openers, so you can study a stronger version of the same move rather than paste in someone else's words.
How does it hook-score my writing?
It runs three transparent rules over your text (in medias res, concrete imagery, sentence-length variance), scores each from 0 to 100 with a plain-English reason, and rolls them into one weighted Hook Score. Nothing is a black box; you can read exactly why you scored what you scored.
Does my text get sent anywhere?
No. The rules engine and the benchmark corpus both run in your browser. Nothing you paste ever leaves your machine.
